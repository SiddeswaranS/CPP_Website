The Excel CPQ problem in Indian manufacturing

Many Indian B2B manufacturers — elevator OEMs, switchgear makers, HVAC system integrators — are still quoting configurable products using Excel sheets that are 10 years old. The file has 47 tabs. One senior engineer owns it. When he's on leave, quotes stop.

This is not a niche problem. The global CPQ market was valued at $2.3 billion in 2024 and is growing at 20% CAGR precisely because manufacturers worldwide are hitting the same wall. In India, where manufacturing is scaling rapidly under PLI incentives, the digital adoption gap in the sales engineering function is especially stark — most mid-sized OEMs have invested in ERP but left the configuration-to-quote workflow entirely manual.

The failure is not a technology gap — it's three specific Excel failure modes compounding on each other:

  • 1 Version proliferation. Every engineer saves their own copy. config_v47_final_REVISED_USE_THIS.xlsx is a real phenomenon in Indian manufacturing offices.
  • 2 No constraint enforcement. Excel has no engine to propagate rules. An engineer can select a hydraulic drive for a 26-person elevator — a physically impossible combination — and Excel will not object.
  • 3 Manual BOM lookup. After configuration, an engineer must manually cross-reference three more spreadsheets to build the BOM. Each lookup is an opportunity for error and each takes 20–40 minutes.

What happens when Excel breaks — the real cost

BOM errors reach the shop floor

When a BOM error passes undetected through sales engineering into procurement, the wrong part gets ordered. By the time it reaches the shop floor or the installation site, the error has multiplied into a full rework incident. For a typical elevator project this means ₹3–12 lakh in scrap, expedited procurement, and re-engineering time. For a large power transformer or industrial press, the rework cost can exceed ₹50 lakh.

The delay is as damaging as the cost. Rework typically adds 4–8 weeks to the project timeline — and in sectors like elevators or HVAC, this directly triggers penalty clauses in the supply contract.

Quote cycles stretch to weeks

Without automation, a sales engineer must manually check every constraint against every selection — and then manually build the BOM from that configuration. The industry benchmark for CPQ completion using purpose-built software is under 4 hours. Using Excel, the same task routinely takes 14–22 days when you account for review cycles, approval chains, and the inevitable back-and-forth when someone spots an inconsistency.

That 18-day average is not an outlier — it is the median for Indian elevator and switchgear OEMs who have not yet adopted a CPQ system.

You lose the order

B2B buyers in India are no longer patient about quote turnaround. Large real estate developers, EPC contractors, and industrial buyers now issue RFQs to three or four vendors simultaneously and accept the fastest credible quote. A manufacturer who takes 18 days to respond loses to a competitor who responds in 2. The Excel bottleneck is a direct revenue leak — one that shows up in the loss column of deals that never close, not in the rework log where BOM errors show up.

Why Excel can't be patched to solve this

The instinct is to fix the Excel. Add more IF statements, add more validation rules, add a macro. This approach consistently fails because the problems are architectural, not cosmetic.

Constraints can't be encoded

Excel has no constraint propagation engine. Rules are buried in nested IF statements that break silently when a new product option is added. There is no way to enforce bidirectional constraints — if A implies B, Excel cannot automatically invalidate A when B is deselected.

Collaboration kills it

Multi-user Excel leads to version conflicts within hours. SharePoint co-authoring helps at the margins but does not solve the fundamental problem: there is no single source of truth for which configuration rules are current and approved.

No audit trail

When a BOM error reaches production, you cannot trace which version of which Excel produced it, who edited which cell, or which rule was applied. This makes root-cause analysis nearly impossible and quality audits extremely difficult.

What a modern configurable product platform does differently

A purpose-built CPQ platform like CPP replaces the spreadsheet entirely — not with a fancier interface, but with a fundamentally different execution model. Four capabilities define the difference:

Frequently asked questions about CPQ for manufacturers

What does CPQ stand for in manufacturing?
CPQ stands for Configure, Price, Quote. CPQ software automates the process of configuring a product from customer requirements, calculating the price, and generating an accurate quote — replacing spreadsheets with a constraint-driven engine. In manufacturing, CPQ is specifically valuable for configurable products where options have technical interdependencies that must be enforced.
How much does a BOM error cost an Indian manufacturer?
Depending on the product, BOM errors cost ₹2–50 lakh per incident in rework, scrap, and delay. For complex products like elevators or transformers, a single missed constraint can delay commissioning by 4–8 weeks — directly triggering penalty clauses in supply contracts. The ₹6 lakh average cited in this article reflects mid-complexity products like standard passenger elevators and mid-range switchgear panels.
Can a small or mid-sized Indian manufacturer afford CPQ software?
Yes. Modern CPQ platforms like CPP are SaaS-based and start at ₹2.8L/year — less than the cost of a single BOM rework incident. ROI is typically achieved within the first quarter. The phased onboarding model (one product family, one plant) means you are not committing to a full enterprise rollout before seeing proof of value.
Is Excel ever acceptable for product configuration?
For products with fewer than 50 variants and no interdependent constraints, Excel can work. Once you cross into hundreds of SKUs with interdependent features — like elevators, switchgear, or HVAC systems — Excel becomes a liability. The inflection point is typically when a constraint violation in one product family has caused at least one rework incident: that's the signal that the product complexity has outgrown the tool.
