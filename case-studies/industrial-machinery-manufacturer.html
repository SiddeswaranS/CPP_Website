The Challenge

Six "master BOMs" for one product line. A 23% mismatch rate between quoted and produced.

The company manufactures custom industrial machinery: hydraulic presses (50T to 5000T), CNC machining centres, conveyor systems, and material handling equipment. Their customers span the automotive components, steel, cement, and heavy engineering sectors. Operations run across three plants: Pune (press tooling and headquarters), Coimbatore (CNC and machining), and Rajkot (conveyors and material handling).

Each product line evolved in a different plant, owned by a different engineering team. By FY 2024-25, the hydraulic press product line alone had four "master BOMs" in circulation: the original Pune design, a "Coimbatore variant" created when the press line expanded there, an "export variant" for overseas customers, and a "recent update" that lived only on the chief design engineer's laptop. No one could definitively state which BOM was authoritative for a given customer order.

The quoting process had evolved accordingly. Sales managers would pull the "closest" BOM from SharePoint, modify it manually in Excel, and send it to the customer. Engineering reviewed the quote sometimes — not always — before production began. The mismatch rate between the BOM quoted to the customer and the BOM used in production reached 23% of orders. Of every four hydraulic press orders, nearly one had a material difference between what was quoted and what was built.

The annual cost of those mismatches, itemised by the finance team, came to ₹2.1 Crore: ₹80 lakh in rework (wrong parts ordered and fabricated), ₹65 lakh in expedited freight (correct parts air-freighted when wrong parts arrived at the line), ₹45 lakh in customer penalties for delivery delays, ₹40 lakh in engineer time for BOM reconciliation, and ₹30 lakh in scrap.

The Solution

BOM archaeology → constraint-driven source BOM → plant overlays → ERP integration. Nine weeks.

CPP's implementation was structured as three sequential phases: establishing the authoritative source BOM through structured reconciliation, adding plant-specific overlays, and connecting to the ERP systems at each site.

Phase 1
Week 1–4
BOM archaeology & source BOM authoring

340 discrepancies found, resolved, and documented. One authoritative BOM authored in CPP.

CPP's implementation team facilitated a BOM reconciliation workshop. All six "master BOM" variants for the hydraulic press line were compared line-by-line. Three hundred and forty discrepancies were identified — conflicting part numbers, quantity differences, and missing assemblies. For each discrepancy, the chief design engineer made and documented the authoritative call. The winning version became the source BOM in CPP, authored across four configuration dimensions.

Constraint Rationale
PressCapacity ≥ 1000T → FrameType = Tie-Rod (not C-Frame) Structural rigidity constraint above 1000T
ControlType = CNC → HydraulicUnit = Servo (not Standard) CNC control requires servo hydraulics
BedSize = Custom → RequiresEngineeringReview = true Custom bed dimensions flag for design review
CustomerLocation = Export → ElectricalStandard = IEC (not IS) Export orders use IEC electrical standard

Four configuration dimensions: tonnage, stroke, bed size, control type. Constraints derived from existing engineering knowledge — no new rules invented, only existing knowledge captured.

Phase 2
Week 5–7
Plant overlay configuration

Three plant overlays applied to the same constraint graph — plant-specific vendors, part numbers, and compliance rules.

Once the source BOM was established, each plant's overlay was configured: approved steel vendors, local part numbers, regional freight carriers, and statutory compliance requirements. The constraint graph is shared across all three plants. The overlay is what makes the BOM plant-specific at derivation time.

Pune overlay
  • Approved steel: SAIL, Tata Steel
  • Local hydraulic component part numbers
  • SAP S/4HANA plant code: PL01
Coimbatore overlay
  • CNC spindle vendors: TIDCO-approved list
  • Regional freight carriers
  • SAP S/4HANA plant code: PL02
Rajkot overlay
  • Conveyor motor vendors
  • GST HSN codes for Gujarat compliance
  • REST webhook → standalone ERP
Phase 3
Week 8–9
ERP integration & go-live

SAP S/4HANA at Pune and Coimbatore. REST webhook at Rajkot. First cross-plant order on Day 63.

CPP was connected to SAP S/4HANA for BOM push at Pune and Coimbatore. Rajkot uses a standalone ERP and receives BOM data via a REST webhook. On configuration confirmation, CPP derives the BOM, applies the plant overlay, and pushes to the plant's ERP — the process completes in under 90 seconds from confirmation to ERP receipt.

Day 63: First cross-plant order configured in CPP — hydraulic press manufactured at Pune, assembled at Rajkot. Pune BOM and Rajkot assembly BOM derived simultaneously using respective plant overlays and pushed to each ERP. Zero manual intervention.

The Results

12 months after go-live

The 23% BOM mismatch rate fell to 1.8% in the first full quarter after go-live — and continued declining. By the 12-month mark, the cost savings were validated by the finance team and an unexpected downstream benefit had emerged.

BOM mismatch rate: 23% → 1.8%

Residual mismatches at 1.8% are genuine engineering change requests — not BOM errors. The 21.2 percentage point reduction represents orders where the quoted BOM and production BOM now match exactly, because both are derived from the same constraint-enforced source.

Quote-to-BOM cycle: 14 days → 4.7 days (67% reduction)

The 14-day cycle existed because BOM preparation required serial engineering sign-off to validate that the quote BOM was actually buildable. With constraint enforcement at selection time, the sign-off queue for standard configurations was eliminated. Engineering reviews are now reserved for genuine custom requests.

Engineer time on BOM reconciliation: 30% FTE → 0

The 1.5 FTE freed from BOM reconciliation was redirected to designing a new 5000T hydraulic press — which used CPP for its BOM from day one. The new press line was modelled in CPP before a single piece of steel was ordered.

Annual cost reduction: ₹2.1 Crore validated

Finance sign-off at 12 months confirmed the full ₹2.1 Cr net saving across rework, expedited freight, customer penalties, engineer reconciliation time, and scrap. The gross saving was ₹2.6 Cr; ₹50L in implementation and first-year licence costs bring the net to ₹2.1 Cr.

Key insight

The BOM reconciliation workshop revealed something the engineering team had suspected but never quantified: the six "master BOM" variants had diverged over time not because engineers were careless, but because there was no single system of record that was both authoritative and accessible. Engineers at each plant made local adjustments to their local copy, with no mechanism to propagate those changes back to a shared source. CPP solved the problem not by adding workflow controls on top of the old files, but by replacing the files with a constraint-driven source of truth that all three plants read from simultaneously.
