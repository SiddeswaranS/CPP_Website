The Challenge

A 60-tab Excel sheet, a 5-day queue, and a 11% error rate that cost ₹12 lakh per incident

The company is a mid-size Indian HVAC OEM manufacturing centrifugal and screw chillers for commercial buildings — hospitals, IT parks, malls, and hotels across India. Their product range spans 50 TR to 2,000 TR cooling capacity, covering R-134a and R-410A refrigerants, air-cooled and water-cooled variants, and ECBC (Energy Conservation Building Code) star ratings from 3 to 5 stars.

The configuration challenge was structural. Cooling capacity selection drives compressor type, refrigerant charge quantity, condenser coil area, and piping specification. ECBC compliance requires minimum COP (Coefficient of Performance) targets by building type and climate zone. Five ECBC climate zones crossed with four building types, refrigerant choice, and capacity gives more than 40,000 valid chiller combinations — and a corresponding number of ways to get it wrong.

Their process: a sales engineer filled a 60-tab Excel specification sheet, then sent it to application engineering for ECBC validation. Turnaround averaged 3–5 business days. The BOM error rate was 11% of orders — wrong refrigerant charge, wrong coil specification, or a climate-zone COP that did not meet ECBC minimums. Each site commissioning failure from a wrong refrigerant charge cost an average of ₹12 lakh per incident.

The annual impact: 6 commissioning incidents at ₹12L average = ₹72L in field recalls. Plus ₹20L in manual engineering re-work on corrected specifications. Total: ₹92 lakh per year.

"In our business, a wrong refrigerant charge isn't a BOM error — it's a site commissioning failure. The chiller gets installed, tested, and fails performance. That's a recall at ₹15 lakh per incident."

The Solution

18 constraints. 5 climate zones. 14 BOM line items derived. SAP B1 integrated. Six weeks.

CPP deployed across three phases — ECBC constraint modelling, BOM derivation rules, and ERP integration — with each phase delivering a working handoff before the next began.

1

ECBC constraint modelling — Week 1–3

Day 0 → Day 21

CPP's solution engineers worked with the company's application engineering head and chief refrigeration engineer in two weeks of intensive workshops. Every rule in the 60-tab specification workbook was extracted, verified, and encoded as a typed constraint in CPP's constraint graph.

The ECBC constraint set was the most complex element. ECBC 2017 COP requirements vary by climate zone (Composite, Hot & Dry, Warm & Humid, Temperate, Cold) and building type (commercial office, hospital, retail, IT/ITES). Eight constraints govern refrigerant selection; six govern condenser coil sizing; four govern ECBC star rating assignment.

Constraint Effect
CapacityTR ≥ 500 → CompressorType = Centrifugal Eliminates screw compressor for large loads; protects against undersized compressor selection
ClimateZone = HotDry AND BuildingType = Hospital → COP ≥ 4.5 Enforces ECBC 5-star minimum for hospitals in hot/dry climate zones; eliminates non-compliant selections before BOM is generated
Refrigerant = R410A AND CapacityTR > 800 → RefrigerantCharge = CapacityTR × 1.42 kg/TR Drives refrigerant BOM line with correct charge density for large R-410A systems; previous source of most field failures
CoolingType = AirCooled AND Location = Coastal → Condenser = FinAluminium Enforces coastal corrosion protection — eliminates copper-fin condenser coil selection for coastal installations
2

BOM derivation rules — Week 4–5

Day 22 → Day 35

Each valid configuration path through the constraint graph was mapped to a BOM derivation template. Fourteen BOM line items — previously calculated manually by application engineers from separate spreadsheets — are now derived automatically from five configuration selections.

Refrigerant charge
Quantity calculated from cooling capacity × refrigerant-specific charge density. Appears as BOM line item with part number and unit.
Condenser coil area
Derived from heat rejection load and ambient design temperature. Drives coil part number selection automatically.
Compressor count
Compressor type and count derived from capacity and load profile. Determines compressor module BOM lines.

All 14 BOM line items derive from five configuration inputs: cooling capacity (TR), refrigerant type, cooling type (air/water-cooled), condenser type, and installation location. A sales engineer makes five selections; CPP produces a complete, validated BOM in under three seconds.

3

SAP B1 integration and go-live — Week 6

Day 36 → Day 42

CPP was connected to the company's SAP Business One instance for automatic BOM push on configuration confirmation. The integration includes material requisition triggering for long-lead items — refrigerant cylinders and compressor units — flagged by CPP's BOM derivation engine based on current stock levels and item master lead time data.

Sales team training: four hours across two sessions. The first live order was configured entirely in CPP on Day 42 of the engagement — a 600 TR water-cooled centrifugal chiller, R-134a, for a hospital in Ahmedabad (Hot & Dry, ECBC 5-star required). BOM in SAP B1 within 11 seconds of configuration confirmation.

Key insight

The ECBC compliance automation produced an unexpected commercial advantage. Post-deployment, the company discovered that approximately 22% of their historical quotes contained configurations that would have failed ECBC compliance checks — but were never caught because compliance was manually verified and routinely skipped for commercial (non-government) projects. CPP now enforces ECBC compliance on every quote, regardless of project classification. This has become a competitive differentiator with institutional buyers who require ECBC compliance documentation with every tender submission. Three government tenders won in the six months post-go-live were directly attributed to the company's ability to provide ECBC compliance traceability on every line item.
