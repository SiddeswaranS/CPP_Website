The Challenge

A wrong impedance isn't caught until the short-circuit test. By then, the transformer is complete.

The company is an Indian power transformer OEM manufacturing distribution transformers (25 kVA to 2,500 kVA) and power transformers (10 MVA to 100 MVA) for utility, industrial, and renewable energy customers. Their products must comply with IEC 60076 (international standard) and IS 1180 / IS 2026 (Bureau of Indian Standards) — a dual compliance requirement that adds significant complexity to every quote.

The configuration problem is multi-dimensional. kVA rating crossed with voltage ratio, impedance, vector group, cooling class, and tap changer type produces thousands of valid transformer configurations. IEC 60076-1 mandates specific impedance voltage tolerances by kVA and voltage class. IS 1180 specifies efficiency levels (BEE star ratings) and no-load loss limits. Vector group determines downstream protection relay coordination — the wrong vector group on a grid-connected transformer can cause protection miscoordination at the substation level.

Their quoting process: an application engineer created a transformer specification sheet manually, a design engineer validated the core design and winding calculation against standards, and the BOM was generated from the design sheet. Total time per quote: 6.5 days. In FY2024-25, four transformers were manufactured with the wrong core cross-section — impedance was out of tolerance by more than 10%. All four had to be rewound. Average scrap cost per incident: ₹45 lakh (core silicon steel + winding copper). Total: ₹1.8 crore in a single financial year.

"A wrong impedance isn't caught until the short-circuit test after assembly. The transformer is complete. The copper is wound. The core is built. Rewinding costs more than building a new one from scratch."

The Solution

IEC 60076 and IS 1180 encoded as constraints. Core and winding weights derived. Dynamics 365 integrated. Seven weeks.

CPP deployed across three phases — IEC 60076 and IS 1180 constraint modelling, core and winding BOM derivation, and Dynamics 365 integration — each delivering a working handoff before the next began.

1

IEC 60076 and IS 1180 constraint modelling — Week 1–3

Day 0 → Day 21

CPP's solution engineers worked with the Technical Director and chief design engineer through three weeks of intensive constraint mapping workshops. Every design rule and standards table in IEC 60076 and IS 1180 was encoded as a typed constraint.

IEC 60076-1 impedance voltage requirements were mapped by kVA class and HV winding voltage. IEC 60076-2 (temperature rise) cooling class constraints were encoded separately. IS 1180 Part 1 BEE star rating requirements were layered on top as additional hard constraints — when IS 1180 is stricter than IEC 60076 for a given parameter, CPP applies the more restrictive requirement automatically.

2

Core and winding BOM derivation — Week 3–5

Day 22 → Day 35

Transformer BOMs are among the most calculation-intensive in manufacturing. Core cross-section, number of turns, winding conductor cross-section, copper weight, and silicon steel weight are all derived from electromagnetic design relationships — not lookup tables. CPP encoded these relationships as BOM derivation rules using the company's design tables and material specifications.

Core cross-section
Derived from flux density, kVA, and operating frequency (50 Hz). Directly determines core silicon steel weight BOM line.
Winding weight
Conductor cross-section derived from current density and kVA. Copper weight (tonnes) becomes a BOM line item with part number.
Silicon steel weight
CRGO silicon steel weight derived from core cross-section and stack dimensions. Direct BOM line with quantity in tonnes.

Every BOM derivation was verified against 40 historical transformer designs spanning the full kVA range. The derivation engine matched the design team's manually calculated values within 1.8% on average — within acceptable tolerance for early-stage BOM costing. Larger deviations triggered a review of the design table encoding, not the BOM logic.

Dynamics 365 integration and go-live — Week 6–7

Day 36 → Day 49

CPP was connected to Microsoft Dynamics 365 Business Central for automatic BOM push on configuration confirmation. The legacy design calculation sheet was preserved as a reference document — CPP output became the authoritative BOM used for manufacturing and procurement from go-live.

The first CPP-generated transformer order — a 1,600 kVA, 11kV/433V, Dyn11, ONAN unit for an industrial customer in Pune — was confirmed on Week 9 of the engagement. The BOM pushed to Dynamics 365 within 14 seconds. The silicon steel purchase order was raised automatically from the BOM line item. The winding copper was drawn from stock on the Dynamics 365 WIP reservation.

The Results

Twelve months after go-live

Every metric moved decisively within the first quarter. By the 12-month mark, the finance team confirmed zero scrapped units and zero rework incidents attributable to configuration errors — the first clean year in at least four.

Quote-to-spec cycle
6.5 days → 45 min

The 6.5-day cycle was driven by a queue of orders awaiting manual IEC 60076 and IS 1180 validation by design engineers. With constraints enforced at selection time, no post-selection validation is required for standard transformer families. The 45 minutes remaining covers commercial review and documentation.

BOM error rate
8% → 0.3%

A 96% reduction. The residual 0.3% traces to part master data issues — incorrect material grades in the Dynamics 365 item master. The constraint engine itself has produced zero invalid impedance specifications or IS 1180 non-compliant configurations since go-live.

Annual scrap cost
₹1.8 Cr → ₹0

Zero scrapped units in the 12 months post go-live. The impedance constraint eliminates non-compliant core cross-section selections before they can reach a purchase order. In the prior financial year, four units were scrapped at ₹45L each.

Engineering capacity freed
2.5 FTE

Design engineers previously spent the majority of their time on specification validation — verifying impedance tolerances, IS 1180 no-load loss limits, and BEE star ratings for every incoming order. That bandwidth is now redirected to new product development.

New product launched
Dry-type line

The freed engineering capacity was deployed to develop a new dry-type transformer product line targeting data centres and metro rail infrastructure. The product line launched 8 months after CPP go-live — a product that had been stalled for two years due to engineering bandwidth constraints.

IS 1180 compliance
100% automated

Every quote now includes automatic IS 1180 BEE star rating validation. Previously, the BEE star rating was checked manually by the design engineering team — and only for utility orders where the customer explicitly requested BIS documentation. CPP enforces it on every order regardless of customer type.

Key insight

The most significant finding from this deployment was how long the impedance errors had been occurring before anyone connected them systematically to the configuration process. The company had attributed the scrap incidents to "manufacturing variation." Only after encoding the constraint graph in CPP did the Technical Director realise that in all four scrapped-unit cases, the wrong core cross-section had been specified at the quote stage — and had propagated undetected through three engineering reviews because no single reviewer held the full IEC 60076-1 impedance lookup table and the core geometry calculation in memory simultaneously. The constraint engine did not catch a new class of error. It made a well-established class of error structurally impossible for the first time.
