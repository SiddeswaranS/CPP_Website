What is CPQ software?

CPQ stands for Configure, Price, Quote. CPQ software automates the three steps of turning a customer requirement into a saleable offer: (1) configuration — selecting the right product variant from your catalogue, (2) pricing — applying the correct price for that configuration, and (3) quoting — generating the customer-facing document along with a production-ready BOM.

CPQ is not a general-purpose CRM feature or a pricing table lookup. It is a product knowledge engine — it knows your products, their valid combinations, their constraints, and their pricing logic. When a salesperson selects a 1250T hydraulic press with CNC control, the CPQ knows that CNC control requires servo hydraulics (not standard), that a press above 1000T requires a tie-rod frame (not C-frame), and that export customers need IEC electrical standards. It enforces all of this automatically, at selection time, without the salesperson needing to know any of it.

Indian manufacturing context: most mid-size Indian manufacturers (₹50 Cr to ₹500 Cr revenue) use Excel for CPQ. A product engineer maintains a master Excel file with product options. Sales managers copy the file, modify it for each quote, and send it to the customer. Engineering reviews the quote sometimes — when they have bandwidth. The consequences are predictable: slow quotes (3–4 weeks), high BOM error rates (5–15%), and engineering teams spending 60–70% of their time on validation and reconciliation rather than design and innovation.

The core problem with Excel CPQ: the product knowledge — which combinations are valid, which constraints apply, which price rules hold — lives in the engineer's head, not in the system. When that engineer is unavailable, on leave, or leaves the company, the knowledge leaves with them. CPQ externalises that knowledge into a system that can be queried, audited, and updated without depending on any individual.

How CPQ works

The configuration step

The user selects product features from a constrained choice set. A constraint-based CPQ shows only valid options at each step — invalid combinations are never offered to the user, so they cannot be selected.

For example: for a power transformer, selecting 33 kV primary voltage automatically restricts the available vector groups and insulation classes to those valid at 33 kV. If the user then selects ONAN cooling, the CPQ knows that certain tank dimensions become available and certain protection relay options become mandatory. These constraints propagate through the configuration automatically — the user sees only what is valid at each step, not the full unfiltered catalogue.

This is how constraint-based CPQ differs from a simple product catalogue with filters: filters remove items but don't propagate effects. Constraint propagation changes what's available downstream based on upstream selections. See our deep dive on constraint vs rules engine for a detailed comparison.

The pricing step

Price is calculated from the configured BOM, applying pricing rules: cost-plus margin, list price minus channel discount, regional freight bands, GST by HSN code, and customer-specific pricing agreements. CPQ does not require the salesperson to know any of these pricing rules — they are applied automatically based on the configuration, the customer's region, the channel, and any active pricing agreements.

For Indian manufacturers, the pricing step typically includes: base cost from BOM (material + conversion), margin by product line, GST at the correct rate for each line item's HSN code, freight by delivery distance band, and installation or commissioning charges if applicable. A well-configured CPQ handles all of this without manual calculation.

The quoting step

The configured product and pricing produces two outputs: a customer quote document (commercial terms, delivery timeline, payment schedule) and a production BOM (part numbers, quantities, routing, plant-specific part numbers if multi-plant). In advanced CPQ like CPP, the BOM is derived deterministically from the configuration — it is not manually assembled. The same configuration, given the same rule version and the same plant, will always produce byte-identical output. This determinism is what makes the BOM trustworthy enough to send directly to the ERP for procurement.

Types of CPQ systems

Not all CPQ software works the same way. The architecture matters enormously for Indian manufacturers with complex, high-variant product lines. There are three principal approaches:

Rule-based CPQ

Works by checking conditions: IF feature A is selected THEN show feature B; IF feature C is selected THEN hide option D. Simple to implement, breaks at scale. Works reasonably well for products with fewer than 100 variants. The core problem: rules don't propagate. Selecting feature A activates the rule for feature B, but if feature B now restricts feature C, a separate rule must exist for that relationship. As products grow, the number of rules grows exponentially. At 1,000+ combinations, the rule set becomes unmaintainable — and undebuggable. Rule-based CPQ also doesn't automatically narrow remaining choices; the user must navigate the invalidation manually.

Constraint-based CPQ

Models the valid state space of your product as a constraint satisfaction problem. When a selection is made, the constraint solver propagates effects throughout the entire configuration — narrowing choices downstream automatically, without any explicit rules for each dependency. Selecting "5-axis" automatically removes all spindle options that don't support 5-axis, narrows the available control systems, and adjusts the tool magazine minimum — in a single propagation pass. This is CPP's approach. It handles 10,000+ constraints without performance degradation, and every constraint is independently testable. See the technical explanation in our constraint vs rules engine comparison.

Guided selling CPQ

Interview-style CPQ: asks the customer a series of questions and selects a product variant from the answers. Good for sales-led processes where the customer describes their need and the CPQ maps it to a product. Not suitable for made-to-order or engineer-to-order manufacturers, because guided selling doesn't generate an engineering-grade BOM — it maps to a pre-defined product SKU. If your product is configured to order rather than selected from a catalogue, guided selling won't get you to a production BOM.

Which type do Indian manufacturers need?

If your product has more than 500 valid configurations, or if your sales team can generate an invalid product combination in Excel that engineering then has to catch — you need constraint-based CPQ. Rule-based CPQ will slow you down as much as Excel once your product range matures. Guided selling is appropriate if you sell from a fixed catalogue; it is not appropriate if you configure to order.

How to choose CPQ software for your manufacturing company

The CPQ market has dozens of products, ranging from CRM add-ons to purpose-built manufacturing platforms. The wrong choice costs more than staying with Excel — because you pay for implementation, carry the operational overhead of the new system, and still don't solve the problem. Here is a five-step evaluation framework:

1

Map your product complexity

Count your features, options, and valid combinations. If you have more than 500 valid configurations, you need constraint-based CPQ — not rule-based. To count: multiply your product dimensions (e.g., 5 capacities × 4 control types × 6 options = 120 base combinations; add optional extras and the number grows fast). If your engineers constantly field the question "can we build X with Y?" from sales, your combination space is large enough to justify constraint-based CPQ.

2

Define your BOM requirements

Do you need an engineering-grade BOM — part numbers, quantities, routing, plant-specific part numbers — or just a pricing summary? Engineering-grade BOM requires CPQ with BOM derivation, not just pricing rules. Ask any vendor: "Given this configuration, show me the complete BOM with part numbers that I can send to my ERP for a production order." If they can't demo this, they don't have BOM derivation — they have a pricing calculator.

3

Assess your ERP integration needs

Does your CPQ need to push BOMs to SAP, Oracle, or Dynamics in real time? Not all CPQ products support real-time BOM push. Some generate a PDF quote and leave it to your team to re-enter the BOM in the ERP. Confirm explicitly before buying: "Does your system push the BOM to our ERP via API, in real time, when I confirm a configuration?" Get the answer in writing. ERP integration is the step that eliminates manual re-entry and the errors that come with it.

4

Evaluate for your compliance standards

If your products must comply with IEC, BIS, IS standards, or ECBC energy standards, verify that the CPQ can encode those constraints natively — not as post-implementation customisations. A constraint that says "for export orders, electrical standard must be IEC, not IS" must be enforced at configuration time, not validated by an engineer after the fact. "We'll add it as a customisation" is a red flag; it means the constraint isn't natively supported and will require ongoing maintenance.

5

Validate with a real product family pilot

Before committing to full deployment, run a 4-week pilot with one of your actual product families. Take your most complex product line — the one where your engineers spend the most time validating quotes — and ask the CPQ vendor to model it in their platform. A vendor that won't do a bounded pilot with real product data isn't confident in their own product. The pilot will surface integration gaps, constraint modelling limitations, and ease-of-use issues that no demo environment will show you.

CPQ for Indian manufacturers — specific considerations

Global CPQ platforms are built for Western markets with Western assumptions. Indian manufacturers face a distinct set of requirements that many global platforms handle poorly or not at all.

GST and regional pricing

Indian CPQ must handle GST by product category (HSN code), freight by distance band (inter-state vs intra-state, distance-based freight slabs), and regional margin differences. A switchgear manufacturer selling into Rajasthan, Maharashtra, and Tamil Nadu has different logistics costs, different state-level compliance requirements, and potentially different channel margins in each region. CPP's pricing rules handle all three natively — GST is applied at the line-item level by HSN code, freight is calculated by delivery distance band, and regional margin adjustments are applied at quote time.

Multi-plant BOM management

Indian manufacturers with two or more plants need plant-specific BOM overlays — not separate BOM files maintained by separate teams. The industrial machinery OEM case study is the canonical example: six divergent BOM files for a single product line, maintained by different engineers at three plants, diverging further with every engineering change. The solution is a shared constraint graph with plant-specific overlays for vendors, part numbers, and statutory compliance — so there is one source of truth and three plant-specific views of it. See our full guide on multi-plant BOM management.

Government tender compliance (GeM, CPWD, Railways)

Government tenders — GeM, CPWD, Railways, Defence — require rapid quote turnaround and BOM accuracy. A tender that requires a quote in 48 hours cannot wait three weeks for engineering to validate a manually assembled BOM. CPQ reduces quote preparation time from weeks to hours, enabling Indian manufacturers to respond to tenders they previously had to decline due to time constraints. Learn more about winning tenders faster with CPQ.

BIS and IS certification encoding

IS 1180 (transformers), IS 2026 (power transformers), IS 3043 (earthing), and other BIS standards carry specific material and design requirements that can be encoded as constraints. When a customer order triggers an IS 2026 requirement, the constraint engine automatically applies the relevant material specifications to the BOM — preventing certification violations at the quoting stage rather than discovering them during inspection. The power transformer OEM case study covers this in detail.

Vendor master integration

Indian manufacturers typically maintain a vendor master in their ERP with approved vendors, MSME/NSIC status, and GST registration. A CPQ that integrates with the vendor master can ensure that BOM line items are automatically mapped to the correct approved vendor at each plant — not just the generic part number. This is particularly important for manufacturers with MSME sourcing obligations or defence offset requirements.
